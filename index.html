Assessing the Impact of Outdoor & Indoor Air Pollution on Community Health

A mixed-methods survey across villages of Punjab — examining health outcomes, economic burden, and behavioural drivers of pollution exposure in rural communities.

Background

Why This Survey?

Rural communities in Punjab face dual exposure to both indoor and outdoor air pollution — a burden that remains largely undocumented at the ground level. Punjab's AQI frequently exceeds WHO safe limits, with PM2.5 concentrations running 2.1× above recommended levels. Meanwhile, 34% of rural households still rely on solid fuels for cooking, compounding household air pollution risks.

Despite growing national awareness, local perceptions, behaviours, and barriers to cleaner practices remain understudied. This survey was designed by DFCA to bridge that gap — conceptualised, designed, and managed end-to-end by Anshika Kansal (Deputy Manager – Programs, DFCA), covering instrument design, enumerator training, data quality assurance, statistical analysis, and policy dissemination to district administration.

Study Design

Methodology

A mixed-methods cross-sectional design integrating quantitative surveys, qualitative field interviews, and direct physical observations. Respondents reflected on experiences over a six-month window (May/June – November/December 2024).

📋

Quantitative Component

Validated structured questionnaires covering demographics, exposure to air pollution, self-reported health outcomes, and economic impact across 602 households.

🗣

Qualitative Component

4 Focus Group Discussions and 4 In-Depth Interviews with Sarpanches, ASHAs, ANMs, CHOs, and primary caregivers. Analysed using NVivo and mapped to COM-B framework.

👁

Direct Physical Observations

Field documentation of visible pollution contributors — fuel use, kitchen ventilation, proximity to highways, industrial zones, agricultural burning sites, and brick kilns.

Core Results

Key Findings

Out of 3,150 individuals surveyed across 602 households, covering the period May/June – November/December 2024:

1

Health Burden

4 in 10

Individuals reported experiencing at least one respiratory or related health issue in the last six months attributable to air pollution. Of those affected, 1 in every 4 required medical attention or hospitalisation.

2

Biomass Fuel Use

70%+

Of households reported using biomass fuels for heating — a critical driver of indoor air pollution risks, compounded by inadequate kitchen ventilation in over half of all homes surveyed.

3

Kitchen Ventilation

51.66%

Of kitchens lacked chimneys or exhaust fans, significantly contributing to respiratory illness and eye irritation — disproportionately affecting women, children, and elderly residents.

4

Financial Impact

34.64%

Of households face Catastrophic Health Expenditure (CHE), where out-of-pocket healthcare costs exceed a critical income threshold. 78% of households earn ≤ ₹20,000/month, leaving almost no financial buffer.

5

Proximity Risk

3 in 10

Households situated within 1 km of a waste-burning site, agricultural field, national highway, or busy road visited a health facility for respiratory problems in the last six months.

6

Awareness Gaps

Fragmented

Younger individuals show greater recognition of pollution risks, while older populations dismiss concerns citing tradition and economics. Many remain unaware of "invisible" pollutants and long-term consequences.

7

Gender & Exposure

Gendered exposure pathways

Women primarily experience indoor air pollution through traditional cooking practices using firewood and cow dung — leading to chronic respiratory conditions, eye irritation, and skin issues. Men face greater outdoor air pollution exposure through crop burning, vehicular emissions, and industrial pollutants encountered in agricultural and labour-intensive work.

Qualitative Analysis

Behavioural Insights: COM-B Framework

Insights from Focus Group Discussions and In-Depth Interviews were analysed using the COM-B model (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behaviour), providing an actionable framework for designing community-level interventions.

Component Sub-Dimension Insights from the Survey
Capability
Psychological
  • Limited understanding of air pollution's long-term health effects
  • Common misconception: "bad smell = bad air" (invisible pollutants ignored)
  • Low health literacy in older generations
Capability
Physical
  • Inability to afford clean alternatives such as LPG
  • Limited practical skills to implement clean practices (e.g., crop-waste composting)
Opportunity
Physical
  • High LPG costs and limited availability in rural areas
  • Lack of viable alternatives to stubble and crop burning
  • Poor infrastructure — inadequate ventilation, sewer access
  • Free health testing available but indirect costs persist
Opportunity
Social
  • Strong cultural norms around traditional cooking and stubble burning
  • Resistance to change ("we've always done it this way")
  • Mistrust toward health workers in some communities
  • Low collective action — "others must change first" mentality
Motivation
Reflective
  • Widespread belief that air pollution is seasonal or harmless
  • Doubts about the effectiveness of individual preventive actions
  • Financial priorities consistently override clean practice adoption
Motivation
Automatic
  • Habitual and deeply ingrained use of chulhas for cooking/heating
  • Emotional attachment to traditional practices and cultural identity
  • Women more motivated by family health concerns; men more dismissive
Actionable Insights

Recommendations for Districts

Interventions are organised into three strategic tracks based on DFCA's operational scope, resource bandwidth, and partnership opportunities in Punjab.

  • Community Awareness & Behaviour Change: Targeted sessions addressing biomass fuel use, open waste burning, and stubble burning — with special focus on indoor air pollution contributors like poor ventilation, mould, and unclean cooking. Coverage for high-risk households near highways, industrial zones, and brick kilns.
  • Frontline Health Worker Capacity Building: Training for ASHAs, ANMs, Anganwadi workers, and CHOs on pollution health impacts, mitigation strategies, and early detection — particularly for vulnerable groups: children, pregnant women, and the elderly.
  • Resource Dissemination & Strategic Communication: Context-specific posters, infographics, toolkits, and training modules for rural health professionals. Coordinated messaging via electronic and print media to debunk myths and increase public risk perception.
  • Evidence Generation & Applied Research: Continued community-level surveys, health assessments, and exposure mapping to generate actionable data for programme adaptation and evidence-based policy advocacy.
  • Doctor-Led Policy Communication: Equipping DFCA doctors and public health professionals to engage district and municipal administrators — using local data from this survey to recommend targeted, administratively feasible reforms.
  • Integrate air pollution exposure and respiratory illness indicators into district health dashboards and routine surveillance systems for real-time tracking and early intervention.
  • Include respiratory risk screening protocols and early treatment guidelines in SOPs of PHCs, CHCs, and district hospitals to improve detection of pollution-related illness.
  • Enforce stricter bans on open waste and stubble burning within a 1 km radius of residential areas, schools, and healthcare facilities.
  • Establish decentralised, community-based monitoring systems in partnership with Panchayats and ward committees to track and discourage burning activities.
  • Promote financial protection measures to reduce Catastrophic Health Expenditure — including coverage of asthma/COPD under state health insurance, district-level emergency health funds, and subsidised respiratory care at public health centres.
  • Mandate installation of chimneys and exhaust systems in all government-supported rural housing (PMAY) — incorporating clean air infrastructure into building codes and design guidelines.
  • Partner with academic institutions and public health bodies for cohort studies and long-term exposure tracking, especially for women, children, and the elderly.
  • Organise pan-district awareness events — Clean Air Fairs, mobile exhibitions, mega health camps — by pooling networks of multiple NGOs and CSOs.
  • Facilitate inter-generational dialogue to promote cultural transitions from harmful traditional practices (cow dung use, chulhas) to cleaner, health-focused alternatives.
  • Research and develop alternative crop-residue management methods in collaboration with farmer groups and agriculture-focused CSOs.
  • Provide waste segregation training for sanitation workers and waste-picker cooperatives in partnership with local NGOs and municipal stakeholders.
